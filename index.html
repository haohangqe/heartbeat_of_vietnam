The Story of Motorbikes and Everyday Life

Vietnam’s Motorbike Culture

"The easiest way I use to explain Vietnam’s traffic rules to foreigners is: “Vietnamese people move like fish.” Fish don’t stop when they meet each other — they just swerve aside. Vietnamese people are the same; they won’t stop just because they see you crossing the street, but they’ll find a way to keep moving forward without hitting you, like two fish gliding past each other. What you need to do is show them where you’re planning to 'swim.' Don’t hesitate or jerk around in the middle of the road — you’ll just confuse the other fish."

(Source: chuyencuahanoi.official, Threads).

Motorbikes have long been the lifeblood of Vietnam’s streets, weaving through alleys, bridges, and boulevards with unmatched rhythm. Motorbikes are deeply tied to everyday life in Vietnam, serving as the main form of transportation for most families. They represent more than just transportation — they are part of the nation’s identity and evolution. Today’s modern cities, the motorbike has witnessed Vietnam’s growth, carrying generations of stories and dreams.

Memories & Experiences

For me, the motorbike represents childhood memories sitting behind my parents with my small arms wrapped around them on early morning rides. Riding gives me a sense of freedom and connection to the city — like the wind, the noise, the rhythm of the streets.

I still remember fun motorbike experiences with my loved ones, or small accidents that taught me awareness and respect for safety on the road. Yeah, you can say it’s riskier than other ways of getting around, but I still love it for its convenience and cultural vibe. Others might appreciate motorbikes as much as I do because they symbolize independence, mobility, and the spirit of daily Vietnamese life. A motorbike isn’t just transportation; it’s a companion through different stages of growing up and exploring the world.

Fun Facts

  • Vietnam is known as the “Kingdom of Motorbikes.”
  • There are approximately 77 million registered motorbikes nationwide.
  • In major cities like Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi, motorbikes account for over 85% of vehicles on the road.
  • Rain is never a problem — people always keep a colorful raincoat under their seat just in case.
  • Learning to ride a motorbike is seen as a rite of passage for young adults — a step toward independence.
  • During Tet (Lunar New Year), people use motorbikes to carry everything home — from flowers and kumquat trees to big gift baskets.
  • Tourists love taking pictures of Vietnamese motorbike traffic — it’s chaotic but somehow organized in its own way!

Reflections

Even now, every time I get on a motorbike, I feel that same sense of freedom and nostalgia. It reminds me of home, of simple moments, and of how something so ordinary can carry so many emotions and memories. The sound of the engine, the smell of the road, and the cool breeze brushing past — all these bring me back to the essence of Vietnamese life.
